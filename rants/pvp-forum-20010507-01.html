It's funny, this reminds me of a conversation I was having with a CS freshman (or maybe sophomore) a couple of weeks ago at gaming night. I was sitting around, drawing data structures so I could visualize them. He came up and said "ugh, structure charts. I hate those."

Me: "well, actually, they're not structure charts, they're data structure diagrams, which are different. But why do you hate structure charts?"

Him: "They're unnecessary. I can carry it all in my head."

Me: "Ah. I see. Tell me, have you ever coded anything outside of school?"

Him: "No."

Me: "Ah. I see."

See, he was saying basically the same thing, only about a different subject. His belief was that structure charts were useless because he'd never had to work on anything where they were truly meaningful, and where they helped him to visualize an immense and complex system, and by visualizing, helped him to understand.

He also missed the point that doing structure charts on small, trivial projects was an exercise. Exercises are designed to improve something: skills, muscles, breathing efficiency, whatever. People training for marathons start out running around the block, and work up to running around the city.

In that sense, dating is an exercise. It's where you learn how to deal with other people socially, and most importantly, intimately. When you're out spending twenty-five bucks on movie tickets, popcorn, and overpriced flavoured water, you're really learning how to communicate with another person on a deeper level. Sure, going to the movies is a baby step, but there's the car ride after the movies, or the hanging out on the front porch talking, making out, whatever.

Eventually, you graduate to college, and from there to what everyone else calls "the real world" (I hate that. High school and college are real, too). Anyway, the point is that you will probably start to realize, when the code gets huge and the feature list keeps growing, that you really need some structure charts. Or UML. Or whatever. You need to visualize it. And those exercises pay off.

Similarly, you'll start to realize that the world is a really hard place to live, grow, and thrive! And you'll start to realize that working together is the best way to pull it off. And eventually you'll realize that working as closely as you can stand with your true soulmate is the most rewarding (and most efficient) way to pull it off.

That doesn't mean you'll never again write code without a structure chart. It doesn't mean you won't go out to the movies alone sometime, or kiss another pretty girl (or boy). It means that you'll figure out, as you go, why these things can be important. The only way to figure it out is to try things out and see what brings you joy, and how you can best share that joy. As the motto of Callahan's Crosstime Saloon says:

Shared pain is lessened; shared joy is increased.

(As a side-note, I'm getting married in eighteen days. It took me a lot of mistake-making to figure it out, but I, for one, see the value of structure charts.)
